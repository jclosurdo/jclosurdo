UNDERSTANDING THE BUSINESS

To get started, I dove headfirst into the business landscape: who’s out there, what they’re doing, and how we can target our own blue ocean.

INSIGHTS

There are LOTS of companies out there that target language learning– apps, online platforms, etc

None of the digital platforms really offer the option to actually speak to another human being to learn the language

There’s a huge gap in the marketplace– most platforms only offer the solo learning experience and either focus on vocab/grammar OR learning through context (movies, music, news, etc), but none of them offer partner/group learning in either of those categories

What the other guys are doing

Mapping everyone out

UNDERSTANDING THE USER

I interviewed a number of 12-15 year olds and their parents to better understand their mental models, then developed primary + secondary personas and several journey maps.

INSIGHTS

They get bored during the summer

They’re at a really awkward age– too young to drive or get a job, but too old  for youth center camps and the like

They’re putting in the effort to try to learn a new language (taking classes in school and using apps to keep trying)

Moms want their kids to spend more time outside and talking to their friends–not texting!

PERSONA DEVELOPMENT

Based on the user research and interview data, I developed a primary persona and a secondary persona– Restless Ryan, the teenager, and Concerned Catherine, his mom. Doing this helped me get into their mindsets and understand their mental models– how they interact with this problem space, what their needs and goals are, and what frustrates them.

JOURNEY MAPPING

From there, I mapped out Restless Ryan’s journey in using Duolingo after nothing was sticking in his German class. Check out all that red– those are his pain points after hitting a wall with Duolingo. He hits a wall and then quits– “why bother?”

 

DEFINING THE SOLUTION

I pulled out a few opportunities from his pain points and asked how I might…

Enable teens to talk to real people in another language?

Manage expectations vs reality when it comes to progress?

Prevent teens from quitting when they inevitably hit a wall?

I pulled in classmates and a teenager to ideate with me. (Ideally, I would have done this with more teenagers, but they’re still in school and this was a four-day sprint– so even getting one was a struggle this week.)

THE CONCEPT

HOW IT WOULD WORK

The idea here is that Ryan would choose some topics he’s interested in and have access to different types of content for those categories. So, for instance, if he’s into building stuff, he might have access to YouTube videos in Spanish about taking apart a computer and putting it back together. Or maybe he wants to do some (safe + at home) woodworking– he could watch a video about building a birdhouse. You get the idea.

Then, he’d be paired with a native speaker of the language he’s learning who has at least one interest in common, and they’d be modern day “pen pals”– which really means that they’d video chat a few times a week in each language so they’re being immersed. Those chats would be recorded and sent off to a Smart People, Inc employee for review. (Eventually, the idea here would be to have this review completed through AI–but we’re talking MVP today!)

NEXT STEPS + CONCLUSION

My biggest aha! moment in this process was that teenagers (12-15) are in such an awkward phase. I mean, I was a young teenager once...and I remember being awkward… but I couldn’t have come to that conclusion on my own. What I really learned from that is how important it is to talk to users– including “peripheral” users. I got that nugget from a parent– a teenager likely wouldn’t have been able to tell me that themselves.

Further Testing + Development

The most immediate next step would be to test the mid-fidelity wireframes– and of course, develop the full platform.

AI Incorporation

The goal would be to eventually include some machine learning to ease the review load off Smart People Inc.’s staff.

SUCCESS METRICS
